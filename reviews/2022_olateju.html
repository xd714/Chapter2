Detailed Experimental Design Flow
1
Foundation Stock Establishment
Strategic acquisition of breeding stock from multiple breeds
Total population: 40 growing rabbits
Composition: 6 does + 2 bucks per breed
Breeds: NZW, DBT, CFW, Rex, Local breed
Source: Reputable suppliers for genetic quality
2
Housing and Management System
Standardized environment for all genetic groups
Housing: Individual hutches with stainless steel equipment
Feeding: Commercial pellets (16.23% CP, 2280 Kcal/kg) morning
Forage: Tridax procumbens and Talinum triangulare evening
Water: Fresh clean water available ad libitum
3
Mating Protocol and Pregnancy Management
Systematic breeding approach for crossbred kit production
Mating method: Does moved to buck's cage
Pregnancy detection: Palpation 10 days post-mating
Re-breeding: Failed conception returned to same buck
Nest boxes: Placed day 25 of gestation
4
Data Collection Protocol
Comprehensive measurement system from birth to post-weaning
Birth measurements: Individual and litter weights immediately post-birth
Pre-weaning: Day 21 body weights recorded
Post-weaning: Day 35 body weights (weaning at day 28)
Equipment: Sensitive scale (10kg capacity, 0.01kg accuracy)
5
Statistical Analysis Design
Advanced mixed model analysis with correlation estimation
Software: SAS 2008 mixed model least-squares procedure
Variance components: Henderson's Method 3
Effects estimated: Genotype, gestation length, litter size
Correlations: Phenotypic and genetic correlation coefficients

Why, How, What Analysis
WHY - Research Motivation
Economic Imperative: Increasing demand for animal protein requires efficient meat production systems.
  • Protein demand: FAO predictions show rising global population needs
  • Rabbit advantages: Low cholesterol, high protein, no religious taboos
  • Production efficiency: Birth weight crucial for survival and growth
  • Genetic optimization: Need to identify best crossbreeding combinations
Knowledge Gap: Limited information on crossbreeding effects in tropical rabbit production systems.
HOW - Methodology
Comprehensive Factorial Design: Systematic evaluation of multiple factors affecting kit performance.
  • Genetic factors: 5 breeds in systematic crossing design
  • Environmental factors: Gestation length (29-34 days) and litter size (2-7)
  • Performance tracking: Birth through post-weaning (35 days)
  • Statistical rigor: Mixed models with variance component analysis
Validation: Henderson's Method 3 for unbiased genetic parameter estimation.
WHAT - Key Discoveries
Superior Cross Identified: CFW × DBT combination shows best overall performance.
  • Best genotype: CFW × DBT highest weights across all stages
  • Gestation effect: 34-day gestation produces 217g vs 66.75g at 29 days
  • Litter size trade-off: Large litters (7) reduce individual kit weights
  • Predictive value: Birth weight strongly correlates with later performance (r=0.78-0.92)
Practical Application: Clear breeding recommendations for tropical rabbit production systems.

Key Factor Effects Analysis
G
Gestation Length Impact
Critical factor showing linear relationship with kit performance
29 days: 66.75±0.51g birth weight, poor subsequent growth
30-32 days: Intermediate performance (72-89g birth weight)
34 days: 217.00±3.00g birth weight, superior growth trajectory
Implication: Fetal development time crucial for viable offspring
L
Litter Size Trade-offs
Clear inverse relationship between litter size and individual kit quality
Small litters (2): 148.75±39.42g individual birth weight
Medium litters (4-5): 90-92g individual birth weight
Large litters (7): 69.93±0.93g individual birth weight
Management: Standardization strategies needed for large litters
C
Correlation Patterns
Strong predictive relationships between life stages
Birth-21 days: r=0.78 (phenotypic), genetic correlation high
21-35 days: r=0.92 (strongest correlation observed)
Birth-35 days: r=0.69 (good long-term predictive value)
Negative correlation: Individual vs litter birth weight (r=-0.19)
Breeding Recommendations
Management Guidelines

Optimal Crossbreeding Strategy:

  • Primary recommendation: Use Californian White sires with Dutch-belted dams
  • Alternative crosses: NZW × REX and LAB × NZW show moderate performance
  • Avoid: DBT × CFW combination due to poor performance

Environmental Management:

  • Gestation monitoring: Ensure adequate gestation length (avoid premature kindling)
  • Litter management: Consider cross-fostering for very large litters
  • Early selection: Use birth weight as predictor for future performance
  • Nutrition: Maintain consistent feeding for optimal fetal development
Success Factors for Tropical Rabbit Production
Genetic Factors
Breed complementarity: CFW × DBT shows superior heterosis
Maternal effects: Dutch-belted dams provide better environment
Sire influence: Californian White contributes growth potential
Local adaptation: Include local breeds for environmental resilience
Environmental Factors
Gestation length: Critical 29-34 day window affects viability
Litter size management: Balance quantity vs individual quality
Nutrition timing: Adequate pre-natal and lactation feeding
Health monitoring: Early intervention for reproductive success
Economic Impact Analysis
Production System Optimization
The identification of CFW × DBT as the superior cross has immediate economic implications for rabbit producers in tropical regions. With 91.9g birth weight vs 74.3g for the poorest cross, this represents a 23.7% improvement in initial kit viability.

The strong correlations between birth weight and subsequent performance (r=0.78-0.92) mean that early selection based on birth weight can effectively predict later productivity, enabling more efficient resource allocation and improved profitability in commercial rabbit operations.
Broader Research Applications
Crossbreeding Research

This study provides a model framework for systematic crossbreeding evaluation in small livestock species. The factorial design approach can be adapted for goats, sheep, and poultry in tropical production systems.

Factor Analysis Methods

The methodology demonstrates how to separate genetic, environmental, and management effects on reproductive performance. The correlation analysis approach is particularly valuable for establishing selection criteria in crossbreeding programs.

Future Research Directions
Research Extensions Needed
While this study establishes the superior performance of CFW × DBT crosses, several areas require further investigation:
  • Lifetime productivity: Extend analysis through multiple reproductive cycles
  • Disease resistance: Evaluate crossbred performance under disease challenge
  • Feed efficiency: Assess conversion ratios and economic feed costs
  • Carcass quality: Evaluate meat quality traits in crossbred offspring
  • Seasonal effects: Test performance across different tropical seasons
These extensions would provide a complete evaluation framework for tropical rabbit production systems.
