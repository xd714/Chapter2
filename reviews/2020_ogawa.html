Detailed Materials and Methods Flow
1
Animal Selection and Population Setup
Established breeding population with specific genetic diversity
Foundation: 100 mice from Japan SLC, inc.
Strains: 25 male + 25 female Slc:ICR and Slc:ddY mice
Age: 4 weeks old (F0 generation)
Genetic relationship: Lowest possible within strains
2
Breeding Design and Mating Protocol
Systematic crossbreeding to establish base population
F0 Mating: 25 Slc:ICR males × 25 Slc:ddY females (reciprocal)
Mating age: 8 weeks
Mating duration: 14 days, then males removed
Litter standardization: Adjusted to 8 pups when NBA > 8
3
Generation Management (F1-G5)
Structured breeding program with inbreeding control
Selection strategy: 3 animals per litter (≥1 male, 1 female)
Inbreeding control: CoefR program minimization
Pairing strategy: Avoid full-sib matings
Population size: 124-140 pairs per generation
4
Phenotypic Data Collection
Comprehensive measurement of birth weight characteristics
Primary trait: Number born alive (NBA) at first parity
Individual measurements: Birth weight within 24hr (grams, 2 decimal places)
Derived traits: Total litter weight, mean weight, max/min weights
Variation measures: Range, standard deviation, skewness, kurtosis
5
Statistical Analysis Framework
Advanced mixed model analysis with genetic parameters
Software: AIREMLF90 for variance component estimation
Model: Single-trait and two-trait animal models
Fixed effects: Overall mean and generation
Random effects: Additive genetic effects of dams
Mathematical Models Used
Single-trait Animal Model
y = Xb + Za + e
Components:
• y = phenotypic records vector
• b = fixed effects (mean + generation)
• a = additive genetic effects of dams
• X, Z = design matrices
• e = random error

Assumptions:
• a ~ N(0, Aσ²ₐ)
• e ~ N(0, Iσ²ₑ)
Two-trait Animal Model
[y₁/y₂] = [X₁ 0/0 X₂][b₁/b₂] + [Z₁ 0/0 Z₂][a₁/a₂] + [e₁/e₂]
Variance Structure:
var[a₁/a₂/e₁/e₂] = [Aσ²ₐ₁ Aσₐ₁₂ 0 0/Aσₐ₁₂ Aσ²ₐ₂ 0 0/0 0 Iσ²ₑ₁ R/0 0 R† Iσ²ₑ₂]
Purpose: Estimate genetic correlations between traits
6
Data Analysis Strategy
Multi-step analytical approach for comprehensive understanding
Step 1: Descriptive statistics and distribution analysis
Step 2: Phenotypic correlation analysis (Pearson's)
Step 3: Heritability estimation for all traits
Step 4: Genetic correlation estimation between traits
Step 5: Selection accuracy evaluation
Why, How, What Analysis
WHY - Research Motivation
Primary Problem: Limited understanding of within-litter birth weight variation impacts on pig breeding efficiency.
  • NBA is economically important but has low heritability
  • Individual piglet birth weights affect survival and growth
  • Need for pilot animal model to study complex traits
  • Gap in understanding genetic correlations between litter characteristics
Research Need: Laboratory mice as accessible model for pig reproduction research with detailed within-litter analysis.
HOW - Research Approach
Innovative Methodology: Comprehensive within-litter birth weight characterization using 9 distinct traits.
  • Population genetics: Structured breeding with pedigree tracking
  • Statistical genetics: REML-based variance component estimation
  • Trait development: Novel measurement of skewness and kurtosis
  • Model comparison: Single vs two-trait genetic analysis
Validation: Cross-species relevance through literature comparison with pig studies.
WHAT - Key Discoveries
Major Findings: Clear genetic basis for litter size-birth weight trade-offs.
  • Strong negative correlation NBA vs mean birth weight (r=-0.31)
  • Positive genetic correlation NBA vs within-litter variation
  • Total litter weight best predictor for NBA improvement (RGG=105%)
  • Selection for NBA increases birth weight heterogeneity
Practical Impact: Provides genetic parameter estimates directly applicable to pig breeding programs.
